# Rise of British India
## Expansion & Consolidation (1766-1818)

Following the acquisition of Diwani rights (1765), the East India Company transformed from a trading entity into a territorial power. This period saw the introduction of administrative frameworks (Regulating Act 1773, Pitt's India Act 1784), military expansion through Mysore and Maratha Wars, Governor-General Warren Hastings' consolidation, Wellesley's Subsidiary Alliance system, and the final defeat of the Marathas establishing British supremacy over the Indian subcontinent.

---

## üìú Regulating Act (1773)

**Background**: Company faced financial crisis and administrative chaos in Bengal following acquisition of Diwani rights

**1773 - Regulating Act Provisions**:
- **Governor-General position created** for Bengal (first: Warren Hastings)
- **Council of Four** to assist Governor-General
- **Supreme Court established** at Calcutta (1774)
- **Court of Directors**: Elected for 4 years, 1/4 retired annually
- **Company correspondence** with India subject to government scrutiny
- Company required to submit annual financial reports to Parliament

**Weaknesses**:
- Governor-General could be outvoted by Council
- Supreme Court jurisdiction unclear
- No control over Bombay and Madras Presidencies

## üë®‚Äç‚öñÔ∏è Warren Hastings Era (1772-1785)

**1772-74** - **Governor of Bengal**

**1774-85** - **First Governor-General of Bengal**

### Administrative Reforms

**1772** - **Revenue Reforms**:
- Abolished dual government (Diwani system)
- Company took direct control of revenue administration
- Treasury moved from Murshidabad to Calcutta
- Established Board of Revenue at Calcutta

**1772** - **Judicial Reforms**:
- Established **Diwani Adalats** (civil courts) in districts
- **Faujdari Adalats** (criminal courts) under Indian judges
- **Sadar Diwani Adalat** (appellate civil court) at Calcutta
- **Sadar Nizamat Adalat** (appellate criminal court) at Murshidabad

**1781** - **Act of Settlement**:
- Clarified Supreme Court jurisdiction
- Government officials exempt from Supreme Court for official acts
- Prevented interference in revenue collection

### Military Conflicts

**1774-78** - **Rohilla War**:
- Hastings helped Nawab of Awadh against Rohillas
- Controversial - criticized in Britain
- Strategic: Secured northern frontier

**1775-82** - **First Anglo-Maratha War**:
- **Cause**: British intervention in Maratha succession dispute
- **Treaty of Salbai (1782)**: Status quo restored, 20-year peace with Marathas
- British gained Salsette island near Bombay

**1780-84** - **Second Anglo-Mysore War**:
- **Against**: Hyder Ali and Tipu Sultan
- **1781**: British defeat at Pollilur
- **1782**: Hyder Ali died, Tipu Sultan continued
- **Treaty of Mangalore (1784)**: Mutual restoration of conquests
- **Result**: Status quo ante bellum (first treaty on equal terms)

### Controversies

**1775** - **Nandakumar Case**:
- Maharaja Nandakumar accused Hastings of corruption
- Nandakumar arrested for forgery, tried by Supreme Court
- Executed (controversial use of English law)
- Hastings' reputation damaged

**1788-95** - **Impeachment Trial**:
- Edmund Burke led impeachment in British Parliament
- Charges: Rohilla War, Benares affair, Begums of Awadh treatment
- **Acquitted (1795)** after 7-year trial
- Exposed Company administration to scrutiny

## üèõÔ∏è Pitt's India Act (1784)

**Background**: Need to establish greater Crown control over Company

**Provisions**:
- **Board of Control** created (6 members including 2 cabinet ministers)
  - Controlled political, military, revenue affairs
  - Could supervise and direct Company's operations
- **Court of Directors** retained commercial functions
- **Governor-General**: Empowered over Council
  - Could overrule Council in special cases
  - Power to control subordinate presidencies
- **Dual Control System**: Company and Crown shared governance
- **Secret Committee**: For confidential communications

**Significance**: 
- Established parliamentary sovereignty over Company
- Lasted until 1858 with modifications
- Created stable administrative framework

## ‚öîÔ∏è Cornwallis Era (1786-1793)

**Lord Cornwallis** - Governor-General (1786-1793)

### Administrative Reforms

**1787** - **Police Reforms**:
- Established system of **thanas** (police stations) in districts
- **Darogas** (police superintendents) appointed
- Separated police from judicial functions

**1793** - **Judicial Reforms** (Cornwallis Code):
- **District Courts** established (European judges)
- **Provincial Courts of Appeal** (3-4 judges)
- **Sadar Diwani Adalat** and **Sadar Nizamat Adalat** at Calcutta
- Separation of revenue and judicial administration
- Europeans subject to same laws as Indians

**Civil Service Reforms**:
- **Covenanted Civil Service** restricted to Europeans
- Indians excluded from high posts (previous practice reversed)
- Improved salaries to reduce corruption
- Prohibited private trade by officials

### Revenue Reforms

**1793** - **PERMANENT SETTLEMENT OF BENGAL**:

**Key Features**:
- **Zamindars** made permanent proprietors
- Fixed land revenue in perpetuity (Rs. 2.68 crores)
- Revenue payable by sunset on fixed date (hence "Sunset Law")
- Zamindars responsible for collection and payment
- Government's share: 10/11 of revenue; Zamindar: 1/11

**Objectives**:
- Create class of loyal landed aristocracy
- Ensure fixed revenue for government
- Encourage agricultural improvement

**Impact**:
- **Positive**: Revenue security for government, created landed class
- **Negative**: Zamindars became exploitative intermediaries, rack-renting, peasant suffering, no incentive for improvement

**Extension**: Later extended to parts of Madras, Orissa, Varanasi, North Carnatic

### Military Campaigns

**1790-92** - **Third Anglo-Mysore War**:
- **Against**: Tipu Sultan
- **Cause**: Tipu attacked Travancore (British ally)
- **Allies**: British, Marathas, Nizam of Hyderabad
- **Result**: Tipu defeated
- **Treaty of Seringapatam (1792)**:
  - Tipu ceded half his territory
  - Paid Rs. 3.3 crore indemnity
  - Surrendered two sons as hostages
  - Lost Malabar, Dindigul, Coorg, Baramahal

## üéØ Wellesley Era (1798-1805)

**Lord Wellesley** - Governor-General (1798-1805)

### Subsidiary Alliance System

**Concept**: Indian rulers accepted British paramountcy in exchange for "protection"

**Terms**:
- Ruler accepted British Resident at court
- Maintained British troops paid by ruler
- Disbanded own army
- British controlled foreign policy
- Could not employ non-British Europeans
- British defended state against external/internal threats

**First Applied**: **Hyderabad (1798)**

**States Accepting Subsidiary Alliance**:
- **1798**: Hyderabad (Nizam)
- **1799**: Mysore (after Tipu's defeat)
- **1801**: Carnatic, Awadh (Oudh)
- **1802**: Peshwa (after Treaty of Bassein)
- **1803**: Bhonsle (Berar), Scindia
- Later: Jodhpur, Jaipur, Macheri, Bundi, Bharatpur

**Impact**:
- Brought large areas under British control without direct annexation
- Drained resources of Indian states
- Reduced states to dependency
- Created British military supremacy

### Fourth Anglo-Mysore War (1799)

**17 Apr - 4 May 1799** - **Siege of Seringapatam**

**Cause**: 
- Tipu Sultan sought French alliance
- Refused subsidiary alliance
- Perceived threat to British interests

**Campaign**:
- British forces led by General George Harris
- Arthur Wellesley (future Duke of Wellington) commanded division
- Combined British, Company, and Hyderabad forces

**4 May 1799** - **Fall of Seringapatam**:
- Storming of fortress
- **Tipu Sultan killed** fighting
- Mysore territory divided: British, Nizam, restored Hindu dynasty (Wodeyar)

**Significance**:
- Eliminated major threat to British power
- South India secured
- Arthur Wellesley gained military experience

## ‚öîÔ∏è Second Anglo-Maratha War (1803-1805)

### Background
- **1802**: Peshwa Baji Rao II signed **Treaty of Bassein** (subsidiary alliance)
- Maratha chiefs (Holkar, Scindia, Bhonsle) opposed treaty

### Major Battles

**12 Aug 1803** - **Battle of Assaye**:
- Arthur Wellesley defeated Scindia's forces
- British: 1,584 casualties; Marathas: 6,000+ killed
- Wellesley called it his finest victory

**29 Aug 1803** - **Capture of Ahmadnagar**:
- Wellesley took fortress in two days

**23 Sept 1803** - **Battle of Delhi**:
- General Gerard Lake defeated Marathas
- Captured Delhi and blind Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II
- Emperor accepted British "protection"

**1 Oct 1803** - **Battle of Laswari**:
- Lake defeated Scindia's army
- Scindia's regular battalions destroyed

**15-18 Nov 1803** - **Battle of Argaon**:
- Wellesley's final victory over Scindia-Bhonsle forces

**17 Dec 1803** - **Treaty of Deogaon**: Raghuji Bhonsle of Berar accepted subsidiary alliance

**30 Dec 1803** - **Treaty of Surji-Anjangaon**: Scindia ceded territory between Ganga and Yamuna, accepted subsidiary alliance

**1804-05** - **Campaign against Holkar**:
- Most difficult phase
- **Apr 1805**: Lake besieged Bharatpur (unsuccessful)
- **Dec 1805**: Treaty with Holkar (relatively favorable to Holkar)

**Result**: 
- British became paramount power in India
- Controlled Delhi and Mughal Emperor
- Maratha power fragmented but not destroyed

## üìö Other Governor-Generals

### Lord Minto I (1807-1813)

**Foreign Policy**:
- **1809**: Treaty of Amritsar with Ranjit Singh
  - Sutlej River as boundary
  - British-Sikh non-interference pact
- Defended India against Napoleonic French threat
- **1811**: Conquered Java from Dutch (returned 1816)

**Charter Act 1813**:
- Company's trade monopoly ended (except China tea)
- Christian missionaries allowed entry
- ¬£100,000 annual for education
- Company rule continued for 20 years

## üéñÔ∏è Lord Hastings (1813-1823)

### Third Anglo-Maratha War (1817-1818)

**Causes**:
- British interference in Maratha states
- Pindari raids from Maratha territory
- Maratha resentment of British dominance

**1817** - **Pindari Campaign** began:
- Pindaris: Irregular marauding bands
- Operated from Maratha territories

**Nov 1817** - **War Erupted**:
- Peshwa attacked British Residency at Pune
- Bhonsle and Holkar joined

**Major Battles**:

**3 Nov 1817** - **Battle of Sitabaldi**: 
- Col Doveton defeated Bhonsle's forces

**21 Dec 1817** - **Battle of Mahidpur**:
- Sir Thomas Hislop defeated Holkar

**5 Nov 1817** - **Battle of Kirkee**:
- Col Burr defeated Peshwa

**1818** - **Battle of Koregaon**:
- 500 Company troops held against 20,000 Marathas

**Final Outcome**:

**June 1818** - **Peshwa Baji Rao II surrendered**
- Peshwaship abolished
- Granted pension, exiled to Bithur (near Kanpur)

**Treaties**:
- **Bhonsle**: Accepted subsidiary alliance, ceded territory
- **Holkar**: Accepted subsidiary alliance
- **Scindia**: Remained British ally

**Significance**:
- **End of Maratha power**
- **British supremacy established** over entire India
- Only Punjab (Sikh), Sind, and some minor states remained independent

### Other Achievements

**1818** - **Rajput States**: Accepted British suzerainty

**Administrative**:
- Introduced **Ryotwari System** in Madras (Thomas Munro)
- Suppressed Pindaris and Thugs
- Extended British territory significantly

## üìä Territorial Expansion Summary (1765-1818)

### Under Warren Hastings (1772-1785)
- Consolidated Bengal, Bihar, Orissa
- No major annexations
- Focus: Administrative stabilization

### Under Cornwallis (1786-1793)
- Acquired territories from Tipu Sultan (1792)
- Permanent Settlement institutionalized

### Under Wellesley (1798-1805)
- **Subsidiary Alliances**: Hyderabad, Awadh, Carnatic, Peshwa, etc.
- **Mysore annexed** (1799): Major portions
- **Carnatic annexed** (1801)
- **Parts of Awadh** taken (1801)
- **Delhi region** (1803)
- **Cuttack, Orissa expanded** (1803)

### Under Hastings (1813-1823)
- **Maratha territories**: After Third War
- **Central India**: Large tracts
- **Rajputana**: Suzerainty established
- Total area under British control: **~2/3 of Indian subcontinent**

## üí° Key Administrative Developments

### Revenue Systems

**1. Permanent Settlement (1793)** - Bengal, Bihar, Orissa, parts of Madras
- Fixed land revenue with zamindars

**2. Ryotwari System (1820s)** - Madras, Bombay
- Direct settlement with peasant cultivators
- Associated with Thomas Munro, Mountstuart Elphinstone

**3. Mahalwari System (1820s)** - NWP, Central India, Punjab
- Village-based settlement
- Associated with Holt Mackenzie, William Bentinck

### Judicial System Evolution

**1772** - Hastings' courts: Diwani and Faujdari Adalats

**1781** - Act of Settlement: Clarified jurisdiction

**1793** - Cornwallis Code: European district judges, hierarchy established

**1801** - Wellesley: Regulations codified

## üìà Economic Impact

**Company Finances**:
- 1765: Revenue from Diwani: ~¬£3 million annually
- 1793: Fixed revenue under Permanent Settlement
- Growing expenses: Military, administration
- Trade monopoly: Lucrative until 1813

**Trade Patterns**:
- **Exports from India**: Cotton, silk, indigo, opium, saltpetre
- **Imports to India**: British manufactured goods (textiles)
- **Triangular Trade**: Britain-India-China (opium crucial)

**Deindustrialization Begins**:
- British textiles flooded Indian market
- Indian handicrafts declined
- Economic drain theory origins
