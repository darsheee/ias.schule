# Early Courts and Legal System (1600-1772)

::: warning EXAM FOCUS
This period covers the foundation of British judicial institutions in India, from Company charters to the first systematic courts. Important for understanding the evolution of legal systems.
:::

## Overview

The East India Company established courts in each Presidency as one of the earliest institutions. The types of courts and their jurisdiction varied until Crown rule brought greater uniformity. Indians held important roles from an early date, and the British demonstrated sensitivity to Hindu and Islamic law.

---

## üìú Company's Legal Powers (1600-1668)

:::details **1600 - Elizabeth I's Charter**
Elizabeth I's charter to the East India Company empowered it to:
- Make laws, constitutions, orders, and ordinances for governance of servants
- Impose reasonable punishment
- Could not contravene English laws or customs
:::

:::details **1621 - The Lawes Compilation**
The Company produced **The Lawes**, a compilation of rules guiding:
- Management of meetings
- Selection of officers
- Arrangement of Company's governance in England and India
:::

:::details **1652 - Chaultry Court at Madras**
First formal court with some interruptions:
- **Presided by**: Indian official and two Englishmen
- **Jurisdiction**: Small misdemeanors, breaches of peace, debt cases up to 50 pagodas
:::

:::details **3 Apr 1661 - Charter Act**
Granted by Charles II:
- Provided for use of **English criminal law in India**
:::

:::details **1666 - Early Court at Madras**
Court consisted of the **Governor-in-Council**
:::

:::details **30 Sept 1668 - First Draft Code of Laws**
Prepared by:
- **Thomas Papillon** (1623-1703), Court of Committees member
- **Mr. Moses**, Solicitor of East India Company

Took effect January 1670 at Bombay, addressed:
- Religion
- Administration of justice
- Types of judicial institutions and proceedings
- Registration of property sales
- Military discipline
:::

## ‚öñÔ∏è Establishment of Courts (1670-1698)

:::details **2 Feb 1670 - Aungier's Justice System (Bombay)**
**Gerald Aungier** (d.1677), Governor of Bombay:
- Established two precincts of justice, each with 5 Englishmen
- Appeals sent to Deputy-Governor and Council
- All appellate trials held with juries
:::

:::details **Aug 1672 - Court of Judicature (Bombay)**
**George Wilcox** (d.1674) appointed first judge:
- Abolished Portuguese laws in favor of English law
- Exercised civil and criminal jurisdiction
- Supervised law and order maintenance
- Judge's salary: Rs. 2,000 (to prevent bribery by excluding private trade)
- **Mixed juries**: Equal English and Portuguese for cases between nationalities
:::

:::details **16 Aug 1672 - Court of Conscious**
Established by Judge Wilcox:
- Free justice for the poor
- Convened each Saturday
- Heard cases under 20 xeraphins in value
:::

:::details **18 Mar 1678 - Madras Court of Judicature**
Under **Streynsham Master** (1640-1724):
- Took over judicial activities from Governor-in-Council
- Sat twice a week
- Tried civil and criminal cases
- Operated according to laws of England
:::

:::details **1683 - Courts of Admiralty**
Charter authorized establishment in three Presidency cities:
- Had power to apply martial law throughout India
:::

:::details **1685 - Bombay Appeal Path Question**
President of Court of Judicature suggested:
- Appeals from Admiralty Court should pass to Deputy Governor and Council
:::

:::details **1687 - Mayor's Courts Established**
In three Presidency cities:
- Consisted of Mayor and 12 Aldermen
- Jurisdiction: Civil and criminal cases
:::

:::details **10 July 1686 - Madras Judicial Reorganization**
- Court of Judicature ceased operation
- **Court of Admiralty** took over
- Also accepted appeals from Madras Mayor's Court
:::

:::details **Feb 1690 - Bombay Judicial Crisis**
After Sidi's attack:
- No judge remained at Bombay
- Deputy Governor and Council handled judicial matters temporarily
- Development delayed until arrival of **Sir Nicholas Waite** (Governor, November 1704)
:::

:::details **1692 - Office of Coroner (Bombay)**
Government of Bombay established the office of the Coroner
:::

:::details **1694 - Zamindari Court (Calcutta)**
Council possessed zamindar status:
- Convened **Zamindari Court**
- Administered civil and criminal justice among Indians
- **1698**: Company acquired zamindari rights at Sutanati and Govindpur
:::

:::details **5 Sept 1698 - King William III's Charter**
Charter determined that Company would:
- Carry out business according to by-laws not repugnant to English law
- Govern trade and officers
- Inflict reasonable penalties and punishments
:::

## üèõÔ∏è Presidency Courts (1700-1726)

:::details **1700 - Bengal Presidency**
With establishment of Bengal as Presidency with Governor-in-Council:
- Company granted full judicial authority
:::

:::details **7 July 1712 - Bombay Council Resolution**
Council passed resolution to:
- Sit two days a week to hear judicial matters
- Ended one-person judicial operations
- **Jan 1716**: Sub-committee of Governor's Council formed for judicial matters
:::

:::details **31 Aug 1717 - New Court of Judicature (Bombay)**
Governor-in-Council announced establishment:
- **Membership**: 5 Englishmen + 4 Indians (representing Hindus, Muslims, Portuguese Christians, Parsis)
- Wide civil and criminal jurisdiction
- Appeal to Governor-in-Council
- Justice delivered from bench, not by jury
- **Officially began**: 25 Mar 1718 (Chief Justice **Laurence Parker**)
:::

:::details **24 Sept 1726 - Letters Patent (Charter Act)**
Re-established **Mayor's Courts** at Calcutta, Madras, Bombay:
- **Composition**: Mayor and 9 Aldermen (7 must be British subjects)
- **Civil jurisdiction**: Appeal to Governor-in-Council, then Privy Council if value exceeded Rs. 4,000
- Practiced English common and statute law
- Served as **Court of Oyer and Terminer** for criminal cases (except high treason)
- Governor and Council Members constituted as Justices of the Peace (could hold Quarter Sessions)
- Granted probate of wills and appointed administrators for intestate cases
:::

## üìã Mid-18th Century Reforms (1753-1772)

:::details **8 Jan 1753 - Modified Charter Act**
Replaced 1726 measures:
- Modified Mayor's Court jurisdiction
- **Key provision**: Cases between two Indians required consent of both parties
- Established **Court of Record** (President and Council) for appeals from Mayor's Court
- Created **Court of Request** for recovering small debts
:::

:::details **1754 - Military Law Application**
With arrival of Royal troops:
- **Mutiny Act** and **Articles of War** became applicable to Company's military forces
- Important for controlling looting and prize money in wars
- Critical during Indian Mutiny of 1857
:::

:::details **1754 - Act for Oppressed English Subjects**
Provided that oppressed English subject by Company's President or Council:
- Could be heard in His Majesty's Court of King's Bench
- Rarely employed due to distance and expense
:::

:::details **1765 - Diwani Grant**
Grant of diwani by Nawab to Company:
- Included responsibility for **Diwani Courts** throughout Bengal, Bihar, Orissa
- **1790**: Company explicitly took control of criminal justice (except Bombay)
- Governor-General-in-Council empowered to create body of law
:::

:::details **1769 - Supervisors Appointed**
To address oppression in interior (Mofussil):
- Company appointed Covenanted Servants as **Supervisors of Country Courts**
- Limited power as no control over Nawab of Bengal
- Improvements in judicial administration limited
:::

:::details **28 Apr 1772 - Hastings' Revenue & Judicial Reforms**
**Warren Hastings** (1732-1818), Governor of Bengal, ordered:
- Murshidabad and Patna councils to initiate revenue and judicial administration
- Position of **Collector** replaced Supervisor
- Ordered arrest and trial of:
  - **Mohamed Reza Khan** (Murshidabad)
  - **Shitab Roy** (Patna)
  - Former Indian administrators of these duties
:::

:::details **15 Aug 1772 - Hastings' First Legal Code**
Drew up 37 rules/sections - **first British Indian law code** in Bengal, Bihar, Orissa:

**Court System Created**:
- **District Level**:
  - **Diwani Adalat**: Civil cases under Collector as judge
  - **Faujdari Adalat**: Criminal cases under Qazi and two mufti
- **Provincial Level**: 6 Provincial Adalats linking districts to Calcutta
- **Appellate Level** (Calcutta):
  - **Sadar Diwani Adalat**: Appeals from Diwani Adalat
  - **Sadar Nizamat Adalat**: Appeals from Faujdari Adalat
  - Capital cases reviewed at Sadar Nizamat Adalat

**Key Provisions**:
- Suits on inheritance, marriage, caste, religious practices: judged by own laws (Hindu or Muslim)
- Civil complaints over 12 years old: ceased to be actionable (eliminated interminable litigation)
- System survived mainly until 1793
:::

:::details **1773-76 - Hindu Law Code**
**Hindu Commission** of 11 scholars prepared:
- **Vivadarnava Setu** (code in Sanskrit)
- Rendered into Persian
- Translated into English by **Nathaniel Halhed** (1751-1830) as **A Code of Gentoo Laws** (1776)
:::

## üîë Quick Reference

**Key Milestones**:
- **1600**: Company granted legal powers by Elizabeth I
- **1672**: First formal Court of Judicature (Bombay)
- **1726**: Mayor's Courts established in all three Presidencies
- **1772**: Hastings creates first systematic legal code for Bengal

**Important Personalities**:
- **Gerald Aungier**: Initiated justice system in Bombay (1670)
- **Streynsham Master**: Established Madras Court of Judicature (1678)
- **Warren Hastings**: Created first legal code (1772)

**Key Concepts**:
- Dual legal system: English law for British, personal law for Indians
- Diwani vs Nizamat: Civil vs Criminal jurisdiction
- Adalat: Court system adopted from Mughal terminology
