# Legal Codification Era (1833-1860)

::: warning EXAM FOCUS
This period marks the transition from Company to Crown rule and extensive legal codification efforts. The Second Law Commission produced landmark codes including the Penal Code (1860), Code of Civil Procedure (1859), and Criminal Procedure Code (1861).
:::

## Overview

The period 1833-1860 witnessed intensive codification of Indian law, culminating in comprehensive codes that replaced diverse local practices. The Black Act controversy, Lex Loci debates, and establishment of legal education institutions shaped modern Indian jurisprudence.

## üìö Language and Jurisdiction Issues (1836-1845)

:::details **1836-37 - Language Study for Courts**
Company conducted thorough study:
- Which languages for judicial system (courtroom and written proceedings)
- **Lord Auckland** (1784-1849) decided:
  - **Hindi**: North-Western Provinces
  - **Urdu**: Bengal
:::

:::details **1836 - Black Act and Macaulay**
See Chapter 3 for details of this controversial legislation
- Related to jurisdiction over Europeans
:::

:::details **1837-38 - Crimes by British in Indian States**
Series of letters from Bengal to provincial governments:
- Established rules for crimes by British in Indian States
- **General principle**: British citizen committing crime in Native State came before British court
- **Exception**: If apprehended within Princely State, tried in Indian State
:::

:::details **31 Oct 1840 - Lex Loci Report**
Indian Law Commission issued report:
- Addressed influence and authority of English law
- Role it should play in British India
:::

:::details **1843 - Abolition of Slavery**
Act V promulgated by Governor-General-in-Council:
- **Abolished personal slavery** throughout Company's Indian territories
- Forbade sale of British Indian subjects
:::

:::details **1845 - Privy Council Appeals**
British Government passed act in 1833:
- Established **Judicial Committee of Privy Council**
- **Membership**:
  - President of Council
  - Lord Chancellor
  - Privy Council members with high judicial office
  - 2 judges from British dominions
- **Beginning 1845**: Appeals from Sadar Diwani Adalat passed to Privy Council
:::

:::details **1845 - Lex Loci Act Enacted**
Made **English law the substantive law of India**:
- Applied particularly in Mofussil courts
- For Indians with no other applicable law:
  - Armenians
  - Parsi
  - Eurasians
  - Portuguese
  - Jews, etc.
- **Consequence**: Left elements of Hindu and Mohammedan law in place
:::

## üèõÔ∏è Court Reforms (1850-1853)

:::details **1850 - Court of Small Claims**
Government of India created in three Presidency towns:
- Replaced **Court of Requests** (established 1763)
- **Jurisdiction**: Cases of debt or damages not exceeding Rs. 400
:::

:::details **1853 - Second Indian Law Commission**
Parliament appointed Commission:
- **Charged with**: Drafting Code of Criminal Procedure
- Based work on draft of Macaulay's Penal Code
- **Recommendations**:
  - Neither Hindu nor Muhammadan law be enacted by British legislation
  - Amalgamation of Supreme Court and Sadar Courts
  - Establishment of High Court system
:::

:::details **1853 - Norton's Pamphlet War**
At Madras, **John Bruce Norton** (1815-1883) initiated controversy:
- Published: *Administration of Justice in Southern India*
- Received sharp counterattack from Madras judicial community
- **Impact**: Controversy reached London
- Acknowledged need for:
  - Codification of Indian law
  - Additional legal education for all associated with Indian judicial system
:::

:::details **1853 - Imperial Legislative Council**
Prepared by **Sir Charles Wood** (1800-1885), President of Board of Control:

**Imperial Legislative Council of India** at Calcutta created:
- **Membership**:
  - Governor-General's Council
  - 1 representative from each Presidency or province
  - Chief Justice and 1 Puisne member of Calcutta Supreme Court
  - 3 persons at large (lived in India at least 10 years)

**Purpose**: Passage of laws and regulations on all topics for all subjects in India

**Indian Representation**: Lord Dalhousie named **Prasanna Kumar Tagore** (1801-1868) as Clerk-Assistant to Council

**Law Commission Transferred**:
- From Calcutta to London
- Reconstituted with eminent English judicial figures
- Men with substantial judicial/administrative experience in India
- **Deadline**: 3 years to complete work

**Commission Deliverables**:
- **1859**: Code of Civil Procedure, Law of Limitation
- **1860**: Penal Code
- **1861**: Code of Criminal Procedure
:::

:::details **1854 - Act IX: Substantive Merits**
Government of India passed act:
- Cases on appeal determined on **substantive merits** (not technical error)
- **Reason**: Indian pleaders arguing orally in English became enamored with technical points over substantive issues
- Attempted to bring flow of appeals under control
:::

## üéì Legal Education & Codification (1855-1860)

:::details **1855 - Law Curriculum Development**
Presidency College at Calcutta and University of Madras:
- Drew up law curriculums
- Step toward formal programs of legal education in India
:::

:::details **1859 - Code of Civil Procedure**
High Courts of India instituted use:
- Addressed:
  - Civil jurisdiction
  - Testamentary
  - Intestate and matrimonial jurisdictions
- **Initiated process**: Replacing Indian with European law in India's courts
:::

:::details **1860 - Penal Code**
Penal Code passed into law:
- **Special treatment for crimes unique to India**:
  - **Thugi** (organized robbery and murder)
  - Professional sodomy
  - Dedication of Indian girls to temple harlotry
  - Human sacrifices
  - Infanticide
  - Suttee
  - Burying of living lepers

**Significance**: First comprehensive criminal code for all of British India
:::

## üîë Quick Reference

**Major Legislation**:
- **1843**: Abolition of slavery
- **1845**: Lex Loci Act (English law as substantive law)
- **1859**: Code of Civil Procedure
- **1860**: Penal Code

**Key Commissions**:
- **First Law Commission** (1833-1860): Macaulay's Penal Code
- **Second Law Commission** (1853): Criminal Procedure Code

**Important Concepts**:
- Codification replacing customary law
- English law as substantive law for those without personal law
- Privy Council as final court of appeal
- Development of legal education

**Landmark Codes Produced**:
1. Code of Civil Procedure (1859)
2. Penal Code (1860)
3. Law of Limitation (1859)
4. Code of Criminal Procedure (1861)

**Key Personalities**:
- **Thomas Macaulay**: Draft Penal Code
- **Sir Charles Wood**: Imperial Legislative Council
- **Lord Dalhousie**: Indian representation in Legislative Council
- **Prasanna Kumar Tagore**: First Indian Clerk-Assistant

**Judicial Principles Established**:
- Substantive justice over technical procedures
- Uniformity through codification
- Recognition of special Indian crimes
- Appeal system to Privy Council
