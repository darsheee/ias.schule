# Cornwallis Code Era (1793-1833)

::: warning EXAM FOCUS
The Cornwallis Code of 1793 was a landmark compilation that shaped British Indian judicial administration for decades. This period saw systematic codification efforts, establishment of courts hierarchy, and development of legal profession.
:::

## Overview

The Cornwallis Code of 1793 represented a comprehensive legal framework that separated judicial from executive functions, established hierarchical court systems, and provided for use of personal laws. This period also witnessed extensive translations of Hindu and Muslim legal texts and gradual development of Indian legal practitioners.

---

## üìú Cornwallis Code (1793)

:::details **May 1793 - The Cornwallis Code**
Legal code representing compilation of **48 regulations**:
- Drafted by **Sir George Barlow** (1762-1846)
- Covered both civil and criminal law

**Appellate Courts in Bengal**:
- Governor-in-Council formed:
  - **Sadar Diwani Adalat** (Civil)
  - **Sadar Nizamat Adalat** (Criminal)
- **1801**: These appellate duties transferred from executive to Supreme Court of Calcutta

**Provincial Courts of Appeal**:
- **4 Courts** established at: Calcutta, Murshidabad, Dacca, Patna
- **1795**: Added Benares
- **1803**: Added Bareilly
- Handled appeals from District Courts to prevent overloading Sadar Diwani Adalat
- Each accepted appeals from 6-9 District Courts
- Consisted of 3 English judges (later raised to 4)
- Provided original justice in criminal trials

**District Courts**:
- **Zillah court system**: Primary civil justice
- **Nizamut Adalat**: Criminal cases of first instance
- **Collector** gave up judicial duties

**Key Provisions**:
- Removed Collector's judicial duties, passed to Diwani Adalat in each district
- Guidelines for appropriate use of Hindu or Mohammedan laws
- **Commissions** (Indian officials) heard cases not exceeding Rs. 50 (reduced case loads)
- Judges could commute mutilation/amputation sentences to 7 years hard labor

**Legal Profession**:
- Appointed **vakils** (Indian pleaders) in civil courts of Bengal, Bihar, Orissa
- Sadar Diwani Adalat licensed Hindu and Mohammedan pleaders
- "Public pleaders" could represent government when party to case

**Administrative Powers**:
- Empowered Governor-General to appoint covenanted servants as Justices of Peace

**Duration**: These courts continued until 1833
:::

:::details **1793 - Law Officers**
Position developed within judicial system:
- **Pandit**: If Hindu
- **Korani Mulla**: If Muslim
- Advised judges of Zillah, City Courts, Sadar Courts
- On issues regarding personal law of Hindus and Muslims
- System later extended to other presidencies
:::

:::details **1794 - William Jones' Manu Translation**
**Sir William Jones** (1746-1794) completed contribution to Hindu law codification:
- Translation of **Manu Samhita** (Institutes of Manu)
- Death in April 1794 precluded completion
:::

:::details **1795 - Stamp Duties on Cases**
Cornwallis revived system of fees in Bengal:
- Stamp duties on cases to discourage frivolous cases
- Fee structure further enhanced in 1797
:::

## üèõÔ∏è Presidency Courts Development (1797-1807)

:::details **1797 - Recorder's Courts**
Mayor's Courts of Madras and Bombay abolished:
- Each replaced by **Recorder's Court**
- Composed of: Mayor, 3 aldermen, and a recorder
:::

:::details **1799 - Madras Legislative Powers**
Act of Parliament:
- Governor and Council of Madras acquired power to formulate regulations for provincial courts
- **1807**: Bombay Presidency acquired similar powers
:::

:::details **26 Dec 1801 - Madras Supreme Court**
Supreme Court replaced Recorder's Court at Madras:
- Jurisdiction: Town of Madras and all British subjects in Presidency
- Scope paralleled 1781 statute establishing Supreme Court of Calcutta
:::

:::details **1802 - Madras Criminal Justice System**
Madras introduced criminal justice in Mofussil:
- **Magistrates and Assistant Magistrates**: Try light cases, give petty punishments
- **4 Courts of Circuit**: Principal burden of criminal justice
- **Chief criminal court**: Governor-in-Council to hear appeals
:::

:::details **1803 - Sadar Amin Courts**
Bengal Regulation XLIX created new level:
- **Sadar Amin**: Higher-level Indian judge for each District
- Could adjudicate civil cases of personal/real property up to Rs. 100
- Purpose: Expedite case resolution, reduce burden on District Judges
- **1810**: Sadar Amin accepted appellate authority over Munsiff decisions
:::

:::details **1807 - Chief Justice for Sadar Diwani Adalat**
Regulation XV in compliance with Court of Directors:
- **Chief Justice** named from Bengal Governor's Council
- **3 Puisne Judges** from Covenanted Servants ranks
- Act authorized Governors and Council Members (Madras, Bombay) as Justices of Peace
- Empowered to appoint British citizens in provinces to office
- **1814**: Chief Justice no longer member of Governor's Council
:::

:::details **1807 - Colebrooke's Digest**
**Sir Henry Thomas Colebrooke** (1765-1837) edited and published:
- **Digest of Regulations and Laws** enacted by Governor-General in Council
- Included:
  - All Judicial Regulations from 1772
  - Revenue Regulations since 1769
  - Legal provisions: Coins, mints, salt, opium, customs, commerce
- First of several law compilations
:::

## ‚öñÔ∏è Early 19th Century Reforms (1811-1827)

:::details **1811-19 - Metcalfe's Informal System**
**Charles T. Metcalfe** established legal system in Delhi Territory:
- Without formal judicial trappings
- Company officers worked informally with local Indian institutions
- Exercised needed justice
:::

:::details **1812-59 - Collector's Rent & Revenue Jurisdiction**
Regulations in 1812, 1824, 1859 (Bengal):
- Returned to Collector jurisdiction for rent and revenue issues
- Subject only to Commissioners and Board of Revenue direction
- Collector's decision appealable only if value exceeded Rs. 100
- **1869 onward**: Difficult cases transferred to civil courts (Collectors lacked experience)
:::

:::details **1812 - Madras Revenue Settlement Change**
Government of Madras:
- Changed from permanent to **ryotwari revenue settlement**
- Amalgamated duties of magistrate and collector
- **1819**: Bombay introduced similar system
- Challenged Cornwallis Code vision (separated judicial and executive functions)
:::

:::details **1812 - Insolvent Debtors Act**
Passed and extended to three Presidency towns:
- Limited possible length of imprisonment for debt
- Previously: Unlimited period
:::

:::details **1813 - Charter Act Legislative Council**
Provided for legislative council to make laws and regulations:
- **Membership**:
  - Governor-General
  - Commander-in-Chief
  - 4 ordinary Council members
  - 1 member each from presidencies and North-Western Provinces
  - Chief Justice and 1 other judge of Supreme Court of Calcutta
- Addressed legal procedures and substantive law issues
:::

:::details **1814-16 - Munro's Village Panchayets (Madras)**
**Sir Thomas Munro** (1761-1827):
- Restored village panchayets jurisdiction
- Limited powers in civil and criminal cases
- Created low-level courts with Indian judges, limited appeal
- Transferred police duties and magistrate office to Collector
:::

:::details **1814 - District Judge Qualifications**
Bengal Regulation XXIV:
- Special qualification for District Judge post
- Required 3 years experience as:
  - Register, OR
  - Joint/Assistant Magistrate, OR
  - Assistant Judge
- Suggested slotting of Company servants in revenue or judicial lines
- Circumstances didn't always allow fully qualified placements
:::

:::details **1816 - Indian Pleaders Regulations**
Bengal and Madras governments passed regulations:
- Licensing of pleaders
- Rules of conduct
- Remuneration
:::

:::details **1816-29 - Superintendent & Remembrancer of Legal Affairs**
Company supported office:
- Covenanted civil servant held appointment
- Handled questions of law or fact referred by government
- Before authorizing legal process
:::

:::details **1820s - Sharing District Court Decisions**
District Courts began sharing decisions/interpretations:
- Through printed circulars
- Formed important additional body of judicial precedent
:::

:::details **1821 - Enhanced Jurisdiction for Village Courts**
Governor-General in Council enacted Regulations II and III:
- **Village Munsiffs**: Cases up to Rs. 150
- **Specially qualified Sadar Amins**: Cases up to Rs. 500
- Sadar Amins could issue own orders and decrees
- Law Officer could decide petty criminal cases from Magistrates
:::

:::details **1822 - Vice-Admiralty Court**
Created in Calcutta:
- Civil and maritime jurisdiction
- Renewed in High Courts Charters (1862, 1865)
- **1891**: By Letters Patents in Bengal, Madras, Bombay, Patna, Karachi, Aden
:::

:::details **1823 - Bombay Supreme Court**
Bombay received Supreme Court:
- Modeled after Calcutta (1781) and Madras (1801)
:::

:::details **1827 - Elphinstone Code**
**Mountstuart Elphinstone** (1779-1859), Governor of Bombay:
- Drew together **27 regulations** for Bombay Presidency
- Possessed flexibility for various legal subjects
- **Main application**:
  - Hindus: Tried under Hindu law
  - Christians and Parsi: Tried under English law
- Remained in force until **All-India Code of 1860** superceded it
:::

:::details **1827 - Increased Sadar Amin Powers**
Bengal Regulation IV:
- Increased Sadar Amin power to try cases up to Rs. 1,000
- On District Judge recommendation
- Reduced heavy case load at District level
- Allowed British, European, American subject as party
:::

:::details **1827-49 - Macnaghten's Case Reports**
**William H. Macnaghten** (1793-1841) published series:
- Reports of cases decided in Sadar Diwani Adalat
- 7 volumes covering 1791-1848
- **John C. C. Sutherland**: Published final 3 volumes
- Another series covered 1842-1860 (appeals in Sadar Diwani Adalat)
:::

:::details **1829 - Insolvent Debtors Courts**
Parliament passed act:
- Established special court in Calcutta, Madras, Bombay
- Relief of insolvent debtors
- Operated with modifications until 1909
:::

## üìã Bentinck Era Reforms (1831-1833)

:::details **1831 - Judicial Reforms by Bentinck**
Regulation V by **Lord William Bentinck** (1774-1839):

**Doubled Mufti Jurisdiction**:
- Cases up to Rs. 300 (from Rs. 150)
- Could not involve British, European, American subject

**Sadar Amin**:
- Cases not exceeding Rs. 1,000
- Could not involve British, European, American subject

**New Level - Superior Judge**:
- Jurisdiction: Cases between Rs. 1,000 and 5,000
- Again non-Indian subjects excluded
:::

:::details **1831 - Bentinck's Magistrate-Collector Merger**
Lord Bentinck's position in Bengal:
- Favored reuniting Magistrate's duties with District Collector
- **Reasons**:
  - Save a position
  - Avoid shortage of trained Magistrates
  - Fit doctrine of Sir Thomas Munro
:::

:::details **15 Sept 1831 - Allahabad Sadar Courts**
Court of Directors approved establishment:
- **Sadar Diwani Adalat** at Allahabad
- **Sadar Nizamat Adalat** at Allahabad
- For service to Western Provinces
- **Began operation**: 1833
:::

:::details **1832 - End of Mohammedan Criminal Law**
Regulation VI:
- Brought to end use of Mohammedan criminal law
- Had continued from time of Mughal judicial practice
:::

:::details **1833 - Charter Act: First Law Commission**
Charter Act provided for establishment:
- **First Law Commission for India**
- **Mandate**:
  - Reform laws of India
  - Provide digest of laws
  - Examine judicial and police institutions

**Feb 1835**: Lord Bentinck appointed first 5-member Commission:
- **President**: **Thomas Babington Macaulay** (1800-1859)
- **2 May 1837**: Macaulay submitted draft penal code to Lord Auckland
- Produced no finished product until **Criminal Code of 1860**

**Law Member**:
- Charter Act called for Law Member as 4th member of Governor-General's Council
- Assisted in formulation of legislation and laws
- **Macaulay**: Served as Council's first Law Member

**British Citizens Entry**:
- Charter Act loosened restraints on British citizens entry (not Company employees)
- Supreme Court of appropriate Presidency provided only source of English law
:::

:::details **1833 - Open Pleader Profession**
Regulation XII passage:
- Office of pleaders open to all regardless of nationality or religion
- **Impact**: Allowed Englishmen to appear before Sadar Diwani Court (particularly Mofussil)
:::

## üîë Quick Reference

**Major Codes**:
- **1793**: Cornwallis Code (48 regulations)
- **1827**: Elphinstone Code (27 regulations for Bombay)

**Key Judicial Structures**:
- Sadar Courts (Diwani and Nizamat) - Appellate level
- Provincial Courts of Appeal
- District Courts (Zillah and Nizamut)
- Munsiff and Sadar Amin Courts

**Important Personalities**:
- **Lord Cornwallis**: Major judicial reorganization (1793)
- **Sir George Barlow**: Drafted Cornwallis Code
- **Lord Bentinck**: Judicial reforms (1831)
- **Thomas Macaulay**: First Law Member, initiated codification

**Legal Texts Translated**:
- Manu Samhita (Hindu law)
- Hedaya (Muslim law)
- Various case reports published

**Key Principles**:
- Separation of judicial from executive functions
- Hierarchy of courts with appeal system
- Recognition of personal laws
- Development of Indian legal profession
