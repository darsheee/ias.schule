# Regulating Act & Early Reforms (1773-1793)

::: warning EXAM FOCUS
Critical period covering the 1773 Regulating Act establishing Supreme Court, the controversial Nandkumar case, 1781 Amending Act, and development toward the Cornwallis Code. Essential for understanding constitutional evolution.
:::

## Overview

This period witnessed the British Parliament's direct intervention in Indian judicial affairs through the Regulating Act of 1773, which established the Supreme Court of Calcutta. The tension between the Supreme Court and Company administration led to the 1781 Amending Act, while systematic legal reforms progressed under Warren Hastings.

## üèõÔ∏è Regulating Act of 1773

:::details **21 June 1773 - Regulating Act Passed**
Revolutionary legislation to:
- Revamp Company's constitution
- Reform Company's administration in India
- Extend rule of law to India

**Supreme Court of Calcutta** created (superceded Mayor's Court):
- **Composition**: Chief Justice + 3 Puisne Judges
- **Qualifications**: Qualified barristers of England or Ireland with 5 years experience
- **Jurisdiction**: Civil, criminal, equity, ecclesiastical, admiralty law
- **Geographic scope**: Calcutta and over Company servants/British subjects in Bengal, Bihar, Orissa
- **Procedures**: Used grand and petty juries
- **Functions**:
  - Court of Record
  - Court of Oyer and Terminer and Gaol Delivery
  - Supervisory review of Court of Requests and Quarter Sessions
  - Admitting and enrolling advocates and attorneys

**Legislative Powers**:
- Governor-General-in-Council empowered to make rules, ordinances, regulations (i.e., laws)
- Must be consistent with laws of England

**Anti-Corruption Measures**:
- Prohibited Company servants/agents from accepting presents, gifts, donations
- Private trade disallowed

**First Chief Justice**: **Sir Elijah Impey** (1732-1809)
:::

:::details **26 Mar 1774 - Charter Implementation**
Charter brought Regulating Act measures into being:
- Supreme Court began operations
- Court's powers brought it into serious conflict with Governor-in-Council
- **1800**: Supreme Court established for Madras
- **1823**: Supreme Court established for Bombay
:::

:::details **1774 - Provincial Courts System**
Company established **6 Provincial Courts** (replacing Collectors):
- Located at: Calcutta, Burdwan, Dacca, Murshidabad, Dinapore, Patna
- Exercised civil judicial functions

**District Level**:
- **Naib Diwans** and **Amils**: Decided small civil suits, appealable to Provincial Council
- **1781**: Civil jurisdiction restricted to revenue matters only
- Remainder passed to 18 new district Diwani Adalats
:::

:::details **17 May 1774 - Slave Sale Abolition**
New regulation provided for abolition of slave sale:
- Difficult to administer due to large number of slaves
- Slavery tolerated in Hindu and Mohammedan law
:::

## ‚öñÔ∏è The Nandkumar Case (1775)

:::details **11 & 13 Mar 1775 - Charges Against Hastings**
**Nandkumar** (1705-1775) sent letters/documents to Governor-General's Council:
- Implicated Hastings in fraudulent practices
- Council majority declared intention to investigate:
  - Sir Philip Francis (1740-1818)
  - George Monson (1730-1776)
  - John Clavering (1722-1777)
- Evidence forwarded to Company's attorney in London
:::

:::details **6 May 1775 - Nandkumar Committed to Trial**
**John Hyde** and **Stephen Le Maistre**, Justices of Supreme Court:
- Acting as Justices of Peace
- Committed Nandkumar to trial on charge of **forgery**
- Based on English law of forgery
:::

:::details **8-16 Jun 1775 - Trial for Forgery**
Trial held in old Mayor's Court facilities at Calcutta:
- Jury required only one hour to determine **guilty verdict**
:::

:::details **5 Aug 1775 - Execution**
**Nandkumar was hanged**
- Highly controversial case
- Seen as judicial murder by many historians
- Demonstrated Supreme Court's power and its potential misuse
:::

## üìã Post-Nandkumar Reforms (1775-1781)

:::details **Oct 1775 - Restoration of Mohamad Reza Khan**
Governor-General-in-Council restored him to:
- Position of **Naib Subah** in charge of criminal justice in Bengal
- Administer Sadar Nizamat Adalat (moved from Calcutta to Murshidabad)
:::

:::details **Apr 1777 - Advocate General Created**
Company created post of **Advocate General** in Supreme Court:
- First appointee: **Sir John Day**
- Responsibilities: Conducting Company's suits before court
:::

:::details **22 July 1777 - Separation of Civil Justice from Revenue**
Hastings separated roles:
- Created Diwani Court for civil jurisdiction at Dacca
- **1780**: Other provincial courts similarly modified
:::

:::details **11 Apr 1780 - Regulations for Administration of Justice**
Governor-General and Council issued regulations:
- Intent to embody 1772 rules
- Reduce friction between revenue and judicial authorities
- Promote impression of justice done
:::

:::details **17 Apr 1780 - Personal Laws Affirmed**
Regulation provided for Indians of Bengal, Bihar, Orissa:
- Continue using Mohammedan or Hindu laws in Mofussil
- Practice repeated in future regulations in Bombay and Madras
- Courts attempted to apply:
  - Armenian law at Calcutta
  - Parsi law in Bombay
:::

:::details **18 Oct 1780 - Impey's Controversial Appointment**
Governor-General-in-Council revived Sadar Diwani Adalat:
- Heard appeals on revenue cases from lower courts
- **Highly controversial**: Placed **Sir Elijah Impey** (Chief Justice of Supreme Court) as head
- Created appearance of conflict of interest
- **Consequence**: Court of Directors ended appointment (1782)
- House of Commons recalled Impey (May 1782) for impeachment proceedings
:::

:::details **6 Apr 1781 - District Diwani Adalats**
18 district Diwani Adalats superceded provincial civil judicial functions:
- Covenanted Civil Servants served as judges
- Functions of magistrate (supervising local police)
- Cases over Rs. 1,000: Appealable to Sadar Diwani Adalat
- Power to apprehend/commit criminals to nearest Faujdari Adalat
- Later: Police function separated from Nizamat (criminal court)
- Covenanted servant provided to administrate police
:::

## üìú 1781 Amending Act

:::details **1 Feb 1781 - Three Petitions**
House of Commons heard petitions criticizing Supreme Court conduct:
- From Governor and Council of Bengal
- From East India Company
- Signed by 648 British subjects in Bengal
- Referred to Select Committee (Touchet Committee)
:::

:::details **15 Feb 1781 - Touchet Committee Examination**
Committee began examination of justice system in Bengal
:::

:::details **5 July 1781 - Amending Act Passed**
**Significantly reduced Supreme Court powers**:
- Separated Governor-General-in-Council and revenue matters from Court's jurisdiction
- **Geographic jurisdiction**: Limited to only Calcutta
- Recognized appellate jurisdiction of Governor-General-in-Council
- Empowered Governor-General and Council to:
  - Convene as Court of Record for appeals from Provincial Courts (civil cases)
  - Formulate regulations for Provincial Courts operations
- **Asserted**: Mohammedan law for Mohammedan cases, Hindu law for Hindu cases
:::

:::details **5 July 1781 - Impey's Procedural Code**
Bengal Government enacted compilation of **95 articles**:
- Prepared by Sir Elijah Impey
- Amended and consolidated pre-existing civil procedure rules
- Published in English, Persian, Bengali
- Set precedent for future consolidations until 1834 Bengal code
:::

:::details **29 June 1782 - Crime Prevention Proclamation**
Hastings issued proclamation:
- Made zamindars, chowdris, taluqdars, property owners responsible for:
  - Precluding crime
  - Bringing criminals to justice
- Fleetingly honored in spirit
- Proved impossible to execute
:::

:::details **29 Oct 1782 - Impey's Recall**
Impey received recall letter from **Lord Shelburne** (1737-1805):
- Position: Chief Justice of Supreme Court of Calcutta
- Reason: Acceptance of Sadar Diwani Adalat position while Chief Justice
- Implications of conflict of interests
:::

## üîß Late 18th Century Developments (1786-1792)

:::details **1786 - Extended Criminal Jurisdiction**
Act of Parliament:
- Made British Subjects in India subject to Courts of Oyer and Terminer and Gaol Delivery
- For all criminal offences
:::

:::details **1787 - Cornwallis' Court System Revision**
**Lord Charles Cornwallis** (1738-1805):
- Reunited in provinces: criminal justice and revenue issues under Collector
- But in separate courts
- **1793**: Changed his mind, separated again judicial from revenue adjudication
:::

:::details **1790s - Transportation as Punishment**
British judicial system in India used transportation:
- As practiced in England
- Sentenced transported to:
  - **Penang** (initially)
  - **Andaman Islands** (later)
:::

:::details **3 Dec 1790 - Sadar Nizamat Adalat Moved to Calcutta**
Criminal court transferred from Murshidabad:
- Indian judge replaced by Governor-General-in-Council
- Assisted by Kajee-ool-Koozat and two Mooftees
- **Jurisdiction**: Infanticide, violent robbery, perjury/forgery, homicide, appeals

**Modifications to Muslim Criminal Law**:
- Regarding mutilation
- Acceptance of criminal intent
- Exemption of females from flogging

**Circuit Courts Created** (for Mofussil):
- 4 Circuit Courts replacing Faujdari Courts
- Located at: Calcutta, Dacca, Murshidabad, Patna
- Tried serious criminal cases not triable by local magistrate
- 2 judges from Company's service presided
- Judges obliged to inspect local jails
- British subjects appeared before Supreme Court at Calcutta (not Circuit Court)
- **1795**: 5th Circuit Court established at Benares
- **1829**: Abolished (unable to award prompt justice)
:::

:::details **1791 - Superintendent of Police (Calcutta)**
Lord Cornwallis created position:
- Maintain public order
- Arrest criminals
:::

:::details **7 Dec 1792 - Police Regulation**
Cornwallis transferred police management from zemindars:
- Placed under magistrate jurisdiction
- Each zillah divided into police areas (~20 square miles)
- Placed under **daroghah**
- These measures also failed to gain desired success
:::

:::details **1791 - Hamilton's Hedaya Translation**
**Alexander Hamilton** (1762-1824) translated from Persian:
- **Hedaya**: Commentary of Muslim law
- Published in 4 volumes
- Great use in administration of justice in Bengal
:::

:::details **15 Apr 1791 - Elimination of Mutilation**
Supreme Council legislation:
- Eliminated Mohammedan punishment of mutilation
- Circuit Courts directed to pass sentences of imprisonment:
  - 7 years = loss of one limb
  - 14 years = loss of two limbs
:::

:::details **12 & 14 Oct 1791 - Police Reorganization**
Calcutta Police corrupt and inefficient, Cornwallis passed:
- Temporary regulations for reorganization
- New personnel selected
- Abolished: Fines, fees, forfeitures, judicial powers
- Established rewards for apprehension of criminals
- Significantly increased salaries
:::

:::details **1792 - William Jones' Legal Translations**
**Sir William Jones** (1746-1794) published translations:
- Muhammadan Law of Succession to Property of Intestates
- Muhammadan Law of Inheritance
:::

:::details **Feb 1792 - Minor Cases Reform**
Bengal Council passed legislation:
- Reduced excess minor cases at Circuit Court
- Authorized local Magistrate to handle petty cases
- **Other reforms**:
  - Small payment for witness expenses
  - Payment to released prisoner
  - Eliminated property attachment on being charged
  - Assistance in prisoner's defense preparation
:::

## üîë Quick Reference

**Major Acts**:
- **1773**: Regulating Act - Established Supreme Court of Calcutta
- **1781**: Amending Act - Curtailed Supreme Court powers

**Landmark Cases**:
- **1775**: Nandkumar trial and execution (controversial)

**Key Personalities**:
- **Sir Elijah Impey**: First Chief Justice (1774-1783)
- **Warren Hastings**: Judicial reforms and first legal code
- **Lord Cornwallis**: Police and court system reorganization

**Important Concepts**:
- Conflict between Supreme Court and Company administration
- Recognition of personal laws (Hindu and Muslim)
- Separation of civil and criminal jurisdictions
- Circuit Courts for criminal justice
