# Imperial India
## Queen Victoria as Empress & Age of Curzon (1877-1905)

This period marks India's imperial zenith, opening with Queen Victoria's proclamation as Empress of India and closing with Lord Curzon's controversial viceroyalty. Key developments include the Second Afghan War creating the legendary "Kabul to Kandahar March," the Ilbert Bill controversy over racial equality in jurisprudence, the formation of the Indian National Congress (1885), military reforms, famine relief policies, and Curzon's administrative overreach leading to his resignation.

---

## üëë Empress of India (1877)

**1 Jan 1877** - **DELHI DURBAR**: Lord Lytton proclaimed **Queen Victoria as Empress of India**
- **Attendance**: 68,000 total
  - 1,200 civil servants
  - 14,000 troops
  - 75 ruling chiefs and princes
- **Duration**: 23 Dec 1876 - 5 Jan 1877
- **Significance**: Symbolized peak of British imperial power in India

## ‚öîÔ∏è Second Afghan War (1878-1881)

### Background & Causes

**30 Jan-19 Feb 1877** - Peshawar talks: Sir Lewis Pelly met Afghan Envoy Syed Nur Mohammed; Afghans rejected British officers on frontier

**1877** - **British occupied Quetta** - strategic point on North-West Frontier

**June 1878** - **Lytton's Arms Act** tightened gun control; Europeans, Eurasians, large landowners exempted

**22 July 1878** - Amir Sher Ali received **Russian Mission led by Colonel Stolietov** at Kabul

**22 Sept 1878** - British envoy Major Louis Cavagnari **refused entry** at Ali Masjid

**2 Nov 1878** - Lytton's ultimatum to Afghanistan (no reply received)

### Military Campaign Phase I (1878-1879)

**21 Nov 1878** - **Invasion began**:
- General Frederick S. Roberts: Kurram Field Force
- Lt-Gen Sir Samuel Browne: Peshawar Valley Field Force captured Ali Masjid
- Gen Michael Biddulph: Occupied Sibi (23 Nov), entered Pishin (26 Nov)

**1 Dec 1878** - **Roberts defeated Afghans at Peiwar Kotal**: 20 British dead, 72 wounded

**13 Dec 1878** - Sher Ali fled to Russian Turkestan; Russian Mission withdrew

**20 Dec 1878** - Browne occupied **Jalalabad**

**1 Jan 1879** - Major Cavagnari met Afghan chiefs at Jalalabad

**8 Jan 1879** - Roberts occupied Khost district

**8 Jan 1879** - Gen Sir Donald Stewart occupied **Kandahar**

**21 Feb 1879** - **Sher Ali died** at Mazar-i-Sharif; **Yakub Khan** became Amir

### Treaty & Renewed Conflict (1879)

**26 May 1879** - **Treaty of Gandamak** (Cavagnari-Yakub Khan):
- British controlled: Kurram Valley, Sibi, Pishin, Khyber Pass, Michni Pass
- British controlled Afghan foreign policy
- British representative in Kabul
- Annual subsidy: 6 lakhs rupees
- **Ratified**: 30 May 1879

**24 July 1879** - Cavagnari and escort arrived at Kabul

**30 Sept 1879** - **KABUL MISSION MASSACRE**: Afghan soldiers and mob killed entire British Mission including Cavagnari

### Roberts' Kabul Campaign (1879-1880)

**1 Oct 1879** - Roberts led Kabul Field Force (6,600 men, 18 guns) across Shutagardan Pass

**6 Oct 1879** - **Battle of Charasia**: Roberts defeated 8,000-10,000 Afghans
- British losses: 18 killed, 70 wounded
- Afghan losses: 500 killed

**9 Oct 1879** - Roberts took control of Kabul

**16 Oct 1879** - Gunpowder explosion at Bala Hissar fort (mysterious circumstances)

**Oct-Nov 1879** - **War Trials**: 163 men tried, 89 executed in Kabul

**28 Oct 1879** - **Yakub Khan resigned** as Amir

**8-14 Dec 1879** - Roberts fought unsuccessful actions near Kabul: 81 killed, 213 wounded

**22/23 Dec 1879** - **Battle of Sherpur Cantonment**: 25,000 Afghans attacked Roberts' forces
- Afghan losses: ~3,000 killed
- British losses: 5 killed, 28 wounded
- **Result**: Afghan defeat

**24 Dec 1879** - Brig-Gen Gough arrived with 2,700 reinforcements

### Final Phase & Settlement (1880-1881)

**Mar 1880** - **Abdur Rahman** returned from Russian Turkestan to northern Afghanistan

**29 Mar 1880** - Gen Stewart marched from Kandahar to Kabul (7,249 men, 7,273 camp followers)

**19 Apr 1880** - **Battle of Ahmed Khel**: Stewart defeated Ghilzais
- British: 17 killed, 124 wounded
- Afghans: ~1,200 casualties

**5 May 1880** - Stewart arrived at Kabul, assumed overall command

**13 July 1880** - Wali of Kandahar's army mutinied, joined Ayub Khan

**22 July 1880** - **British recognized Abdur Rahman as Amir of Kabul**

**27 July 1880** - **BATTLE OF MAIWAND** - British disaster:
- Brig-Gen Burrows' 2,500 vs Ayub Khan's 15,000
- **British losses**: 971 killed, 168 wounded/missing
- Afghans victorious

**28 July 1880** - Gen Primrose withdrew into Kandahar citadel

**6 Aug 1880** - Disastrous sortie from Kandahar: 99 killed, 109 wounded

**8-24 Aug 1880** - Yakub Khan besieged Kandahar

**11-31 Aug 1880** - **ROBERTS' FAMOUS MARCH**:
- **Kabul to Kandahar: 334 miles in 21 days**
- Force: ~10,000 men
- **Legendary feat** in military history

**1 Sept 1880** - **Battle of Mazra** (near Kandahar): Roberts defeated Yakub Khan's 20,000
- British: 35 killed, 213 wounded
- Afghans: ~1,200 dead

**11 Sept 1880** - Stewart evacuated Kabul

**15 Apr 1881** - Last British forces left Kandahar (turned over to Abdur Rahman 21 Apr)

## üåæ Great South India Famine (1876-1878)

**1876-78** - Famine impacted South India:
- Half of Madras Presidency
- Mysore
- Part of Hyderabad
- Deccan districts of Bombay Presidency
- **Deaths**: Over 5 million

**24 Feb 1876** - Secretary of State reduced ICS exam age to 19 - effectively barred most Indian candidates

**12 Apr 1876** - **Lord Lytton** assumed Viceroyalty

### Famine Relief Measures

**Jan 1877** - Sir Richard Temple appointed Commissioner to Madras for famine relief

**Aug 1877-1878** - Famine extended to more provinces, impacting ~36 million people

**17 Aug-27 Sept 1877** - Lytton toured Madras and Mysore to investigate famine

**May 1878** - **Famine Commission appointed**:
- President: Sir Richard Strachey
- Secretary: Sir Charles A. Elliott
- Mission: Investigate impact, suggest relief programs, outline government role

**July 1880** - Commission issued report with principles for future famines

**Famine Insurance Fund created**:
- ¬£1,000,000 per year set aside
- ¬£500,000: Railway/public works
- ¬£250,000: Irrigation projects

## üèõÔ∏è Lord Ripon's Reforms (1880-1884)

**8 June 1880** - **Lord Ripon** assumed Viceroyalty at Simla

### Factory Legislation (1881)

**13 Mar 1881** - **First Factory Act** passed:
- Children under 12: Max 9 hours/day
- Children under 7: Forbidden in factories with 100+ employees using mechanical power
- 1-hour break during shift required
- 4 holidays per month
- Local government inspection
- Fines up to Rs. 200 for violations

### Local Self-Government (1882)

**18 May 1882** - Ripon introduced resolution for **local self-government**:
- Municipal, district, local boards
- Local control of rates and taxes
- Preponderance of elected Indian non-official members

**Late 1884** - Most provinces passed Local Self-Government Acts
- **Success**: Local duties discharged effectively
- Indian political education developed

### Restoration of Princely Rule

**1881** - **Mysore restored** to adopted son of deposed Raja (previously taken over 1830)

## ‚öñÔ∏è Ilbert Bill Controversy (1883-1884)

### Background

**2 Feb 1883** - Law Member **Sir Courtenay Ilbert** introduced bill: Indian Sessions Judge or District Magistrate could try British subjects on criminal charges

### Opposition Erupts

**28 Feb 1883** - **Calcutta Town Hall meeting** opposed bill - racist speeches delivered
- European and Anglo-Indian Association formed
- Eurasian Association formed

**29 Mar 1883** - **European and Anglo-Indian Defence Association** formed at Calcutta
- Anglo-Indian press (Englishman newspaper) published hysterical articles
- Focus: "Danger to European women under Indian judges"
- Opposition from: Calcutta bar, Bengal planters, non-official British

**9 Mar 1883** - Imperial Legislative Council debated - **insufficient support**

### Compromise

**10 Aug 1883** - Turner Compromise considered: limit jurisdiction, refer cases to High Courts

**21 Dec 1883** - **Final compromise**:
- European defendant could claim jury with at least half European members
- No distinction between European/Indian magistrate assignment
- District Magistrate powers: up to 6 months imprisonment and/or Rs. 2,000 fine

**25 Jan 1884** - **Ilbert Bill amended and passed**

## üèõÔ∏è Administrative Reforms (1886-1892)

### Public Services Commission (1886-1892)

**June 1886** - Dufferin appointed **Public Services Commission** (15 members, 6 Indians)
- Chairman: Sir Charles Aitchison

**Jan 1888** - Commission recommendations:
- Rejected simultaneous exams in London and India
- Raised maximum ICS entry age: 19 to 23 years
- Statutory Civil Service members could remain
- Provincial Civil Service: 93 ICS appointments
- Provincial appointments in: Archaeological, Education, Forest, Police, Postal, Public Works

**1892** - Provisions adopted

### Indian Councils Act (1892)

**1892** - **Parliament passed Indian Councils Act**:
- Increased non-official members on Viceroy's Legislative Council: 10-16
- Provincial councils (Bengal, Madras, Bombay, NWP & Oudh): 8-20 members
- Right to examine budget, discuss finance
- Members indirectly elected (nominated by corporate bodies)
- Official majority maintained

## üíä Social Reform Movements

### Temperance Movement (1888-1889)

**25 Mar 1888** - House of Commons motion charged Government of India with selling alcohol for revenue

**30 Apr 1889** - Samuel Smith's resolution condemned excise policy on spirits

**Result**: Lord Lansdowne abolished out-still system, extended central distillery system in Bengal

### Opium Trade Controversy (1888-1895)

**1888** - **Society for Suppression of Opium Trade** founded in London
- Leaders: Joseph Pease, William Caine, Samuel Smith
- Demanded closure of opium dens

**1891** - Government of India reforms:
- Abolished "minimum vend" system in Bombay
- Illegal to use opium in licensed shops
- Limited opium exports
- Sought Indian States' help against illicit trade
- Baluchistan: government control
- Burma: users registered

**10 Apr 1891** - House of Commons declared opium revenue system "immoral"

**1893** - **Royal Commission on Opium** appointed under Lord Brassey
- Toured India gathering data (Nov 1893)

**Apr 1895** - Report concluded: **no changes required** to opium system or revenues

## üåæ Western India Famine (1896-1898)

**1896** - Monsoon failure in Western India led to severe drought

**By end of 1896**: 3.5 million Indians on famine relief
- **Affected areas**: Bombay Presidency, western India, parts of Rajputana, Central India, south-east Punjab
- **Relief measures**: Relief work projects, poor houses, remission/suspension of land revenue

**Jan 1897** - **Indian Charitable Relief Fund** established at Calcutta
- Received gifts from across India and worldwide

**Dec 1897** - **Lord Elgin appointed Indian Famine Commission**
- Chairman: Sir James B. Lyall (1838-1916)
- Task: Review existing Famine Codes based on current experiences

**Oct 1898** - Commission's report:
- Affirmed existing Codes' practices
- Updated public works relief projects
- Recommended additional provisions for weavers, forest-tribes, aboriginal hill tribes

## ü¶† Bubonic Plague (1896-1897)

**July 1896** - **Bubonic plague first reported in Bombay**
- Source: Rats on ships from Hong Kong
- Dr. Waldemar Haffkine (1860-1930) diagnosed and initiated anti-plague vaccine (mixed results)

**10 May 1897** - Hindu and Muslim memorial to Governor of Bombay warned of invasive British sanitation measures violating religious practices

**22 June 1897** - **Chapekar Brothers' Assassination**:
- Damodar and Balkrishna Chapekar murdered Walter C. Rand (Chairman, Bombay Plague Committee) and Lt. Charles E. Ayerst at Poona
- **Bal Gangadhar Tilak** charged with sedition for newspaper editorials in *Kesari*
- Sentenced to 18 months imprisonment

**2 Oct 1897** - Plague Commission formed in Bombay (Andrew Wingate presiding)

**13 Oct 1897** - Scientific Committee appointed to study plague and drug responses
- Measures: House inspection/disinfection, isolation at hospitals, travel restrictions, ship quarantine

**Mar 1898** - Muslims rioted in Bombay over anti-plague restrictions

**21 May 1898** - Disturbances in Calcutta

**Sept 1898** - Lord Sandhurst (Governor of Bombay) terminated quarantine system, substituted less invasive measures

**Death toll**: ~20,000 in Bombay during this outbreak

## üëë Lord Curzon Era (1899-1905)

**6 Jan 1899** - **Lord Curzon** (1859-1925) assumed Viceroyalty

### Persian Gulf Policy (1899)

**Jan 1899** - Curzon instructed Colonel Meade to conclude agreement with Sheikh Mubarak of Kuwait
- Preclude cession of land to foreign powers (especially Germany)

**16 Feb 1899** - British Admiral Douglas threatened Sultan of Oman
- Demanded termination of French coaling station agreement at Bunder Jissah
- Sultan complied under threat of Muscat bombardment

**16 Nov 1903** - Curzon toured Persian Gulf: Muscat, Sharjah, Bandar Abbas, Kuwait, Bushire
- Demonstrated British naval supremacy
- British Navy familiarized with Gulf ports

### Famine Crisis (1900-1905)

**1900** - Famine, cholera, plague impacted 60 million Indians
- Curzon became **first Viceroy to tour famine-stricken area**
- By spring 1900: Government providing 5 million with famine relief

**1901** - **Famine Commission** appointed (President: Sir Colin C. Scott-Moncrieff)
- Inaugurated irrigation projects: 6.5 million acres, cost ¬£30 million

**1896-1905 Total Impact**:
- **Deaths**: ~8 million Indians (famine, cholera, plague)
- **Costs**:
  - Direct relief: ¬£6,670,000
  - Loans and advances: ¬£1,585,000
  - Land revenue remitted: ¬£1,333,000
  - Loans to Native States: ¬£1,800,000
  - Relief/lost revenue in Native States: ¬£4,000,000

### Foreign Military Actions

**13 June 1900** - **Boxer Rebellion (China)**:
- Indian Army sent ~3,000 troops to international force
- Units: 1st Sikhs, 24th Punjab Infantry, 7th Rajputs, Bengal Lancers
- **14 Aug 1900**: 1st Sikh Regiment helped raise 55-day siege of foreign legations at Peking

**Sept 1899** - **South African War support**:
- Forwarded 13,200 British troops, 9,000 Indians (non-combatants)
- Lumsden's Horse (European Volunteer Contingent) raised
- Arrived in time to hold Natal for British

**1 Dec 1900** - Mahsud tribe blockade for raids/robberies
- Passive blockade with punitive sallies
- **10 Mar 1902**: Temporary peace negotiated (lasted ~5 years)

### Administrative Reforms

**6 Feb 1901** - **Victoria Memorial Hall** initiated at Calcutta Town Hall
- White marble structure as historical/national gallery
- Memorial to Queen Victoria, symbol of British Raj
- Curzon raised ¬£400,000 of ¬£5 million cost
- Designer: Sir William Emerson (1843-1924)
- Foundation stone: Jan 1906 (Prince of Wales)
- **Completed**: Dec 1921 (delayed by WWI)

**4 June 1901** - **Imperial Cadet Corps** established
- Military training for elite young men from Princely/noble families
- First step toward Indian officers in Indian Army
- 1910: Under 20 members

**7 June 1901** - **Indian Famine Union** launched in London (Leonard Courtney)
- Study famine causes and remedies
- William Wedderburn's recommendations: elastic revenue collection, agriculture banks, "takav" loans

**25 July 1901** - Curzon forwarded Gandhi's letter on Indian emigrants in South Africa
- Addressed indentured servitude conditions
- Later pressure from Gokhale, Hardinge, Rev. Andrews ended practice

**9 Nov 1901** - **North-West Frontier Province created** from Punjab:
- Districts: Hazara, Peshawar, Kohat, Bannu, Dera Ismail Khan
- Agencies: Khyber, Malakand, Kurram, Tochi, Wana
- Reported directly to Viceroy
- First Chief Commissioner: Lt-Col Sir Harold A. Deane (1854-1908)

**Oct 1899** - **Frontier tribal policy**:
- Paid Pathans, Afridis, other tribes to police own territories
- Organized levies: Khyber Rifles, Samana Rifles, Kurram Militia
- British forces reduced by 11,000 to 4,000 at key centers
- Tribes received annual payment

### Currency & Trade

**20 Mar 1899** - Act for countervailing duties on sugar

**Sept 1899** - **Indian Coinage and Paper Currency Act** (Sir Clinton Dawkins)
- Made sovereign legal tender at Rs. 15 = 1 sovereign
- Stabilized currency exchange

**1898** - Indian Currency Committee appointed (Chairman: Sir Henry Fowler)

### Coronation Durbar (1901-1902)

**29 Dec 1901-9 Jan 1902** - **Coronation Durbar at Delhi** (King Edward VII)
- Spectacular success despite criticism of expense
- **1 Jan 1902**: Honored 300+ Indian Mutiny veterans

**Mar 1902** - Agreement with Nizam of Hyderabad:
- British lease Berar perpetually for ¬£167,000 annual rent
- Nizam's sovereignty affirmed
- Hyderabad Contingent incorporated into Indian Army
- Nizam reduced irregular army, used rent to liquidate liabilities

### Police & Security

**1902** - **Indian Police Commission** (Chairman: Sir Andrew Fraser)
- Report (30 May 1903): Severely criticized police system
- Reforms: Greater efficiency, better training, eliminate corruption, increase pay
- Created **Department of Criminal Intelligence** (Director: Sir Harold A. Stuart)

**18 Feb 1898** - **Indian Penal Code (Amendment) Bill**: Curbed sedition in vernacular press over plague/famine handling

### Curzon-Kitchener Conflict (1902-1905)

**28 Nov 1902** - Lord Kitchener (1850-1916) arrived as Commander-in-Chief

**Feb 1903** - Kitchener proposed reducing Military Member's powers
- Objected to 1861 Indian Councils Act command structure
- Curzon rejected, asked him to study system for a year

**May 1903** - Kitchener proposed Military Department under his orders
- Curzon rejected; Kitchener threatened resignation

**Apr 1904** - Kitchener submitted reorganization paper directly to Imperial Defence Committee (bypassing Viceroy)

**Sept 1904** - Kitchener offered resignation to Acting Viceroy Lord Ampthill
- Convinced to withdraw

**12 Jan 1905** - St. John Brodrick (Secretary of State) suggested independent commission

**Apr 1905** - Kitchener organized press campaign in *The Times* and *Standard*

**30 May 1905** - Cabinet approved eliminating Military Member position
- Duties distributed to Commander-in-Chief and new Military Supply Member

**12 Aug 1905** - **Curzon resigned** over Military Supply Member selection dispute
- His recommendation (Gen. Sir Edmund Barrow) denied

**16 Aug 1905** - Balfour accepted Curzon's resignation

**21 Aug 1905** - Lord Minto (1845-1914) selected as successor

**19 Mar 1906** - Kitchener's reorganization formally implemented

### Tibet & Younghusband Mission (1903-1904)

**Background**: Tensions over Tibetan missions to Czar, Russian agent Agvan Dorzhiev in Tibet, Dalai Lama refusing Curzon's letters

**8 Jan 1903** - Government urged forwarding British Mission to Lhasa

**18 July 1903** - Brigadier-General Francis Younghusband (1863-1942) arrived at Khamba Jong with escort
- Negotiations failed over 5 months

**11 Dec 1903** - Mission proceeded to Gyantse with 2,000 Gurkhas (Brig-Gen James MacDonald)

**31 Mar 1904** - Encountered 2,000 Tibetan troops near Guru
- Firing: 840 Tibetans killed/wounded

**11 Apr 1904** - Reached Gyantse
- **5 May**: Attacked, fought off 240 Tibetan casualties
- **6 May**: Drove 3,000 Tibetans from Karo La Gorge
- **6 July**: Captured Tibetan fort at Gyantse

**3 Aug 1904** - Entered Lhasa unopposed (Dalai Lama fled)

**7 Sept 1904** - **Treaty of Lhasa**:
- British Trade Agent at Gyantse with right to visit Lhasa
- British occupation of Chumbi Valley
- Tibetan indemnity: Rs. 25 lakhs (75 years)
- London reduced to Rs. 25 lakhs (3 years); rebuke for exceeding instructions

**23 Sept 1904** - Younghusband left Lhasa

**Jan 1908** - British evacuated Chumbi Valley after final indemnity payment

### Bengal Partition Prelude (1903-1905)

**7 Dec 1903** - Initial planning for Bengal partition
- Transfer Chittagong, Dacca, Mymensingh, Tippera Hills to Assam
- New province: East Bengal and Assam (Muslim majority)
- Religious vs ethnic basis
- 1904: Torrent of objections failed to move Curzon

**2 Feb 1905** - Proposals submitted to Secretary of State

**9 June 1905** - Brodrick approved with minor modifications

**19 July 1905** - Government formally passed partition resolution

**16 Oct 1905** - **Partition executed**:
- East Bengal and Assam: 18 million Muslims, 12 million Hindus
- Provided Congress with major rallying issue

### Other Initiatives

**Mar 1904** - **Cooperative Societies Act**:
- Aid rural finance
- By 1911: 3,456 societies, capital ¬£686,000

**1903-05** - Sir Henry McMahon (1862-1949) settled Persia-Afghanistan boundary

**12 Dec 1904** - Sir Louis Dane negotiated new Afghan treaty
- **21 Mar 1905**: Concluded, reiterated prior agreements with Abdur Rahman

**Mar 1905** - **Department of Commerce and Industry** created
- Seat on Viceroy's Executive Council
- Activities: Railways, post/telegraph, ports, shipping, mining, customs
- Director: Sir John P. Hewett (1854-1941)

**1905** - **Imperial Customs Service** created
- Regularized officer appointments, increased expertise
- Praised by business community

**15 Nov 1905** - Curzon left India after 90-day extension to receive Prince and Princess of Wales
