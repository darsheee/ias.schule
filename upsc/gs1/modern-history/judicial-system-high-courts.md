# High Courts & Modern Era (1860-1947)

::: warning EXAM FOCUS
This period covers the establishment of High Courts (1861), the controversial Ilbert Bill (1882), extensive legislation by Maine and Stephen, and the gradual Indianization of the legal profession leading to independence. Critical for understanding modern Indian judiciary.
:::

## Overview

The Indian High Courts Act of 1861 established unified High Courts replacing Supreme Courts and Sadar Adalats. This period witnessed massive legislative activity by Law Members Sir Henry Maine and Sir James Fitzjames Stephen, the Ilbert Bill controversy revealing racial tensions, and steady advancement of Indians in the legal profession.

## üèõÔ∏è Establishment of High Courts (1861)

:::details **6 Aug 1861 - Indian High Courts Act**
Established High Court of Judicature in Calcutta, Madras, Bombay:

**Replaced**:
- Former Supreme Courts
- Sadar Adalats

**Jurisdiction**:
- Civil
- Criminal
- Admiralty and vice-admiralty
- Testamentary
- Intestate and matrimonial law

**Body of Law Applied**:
- Acts of Parliament
- Orders in Council
- Charters
- Acts of Legislature (India)
- Prior judgements of courts

**Composition**:
- Chief Justice
- Not more than 15 Puisne Judges

**Powers**:
- Established own rules for admission of lawyers to bar

**Commencement**: 28 Dec 1865 - High Courts went into session
:::

:::details **1863 - Recorder's Courts in Burma**
Government of India established:
- Recorder's Courts at Rangoon and Moulmein
- Barrister/advocate of 5 years experience presided
- Appeal to High Court of Bengal possible
- **1872**: Judicial commissioner established in Burma (defacto High Court of Burma)
:::

:::details **Oct 1863 - Indian Vakils in High Courts**
High Court of Madras passed new rules:
- Allowed **Indian vakils to argue cases** in High Court on original side
- Led way for same grant in Calcutta and Bombay High Courts
:::

:::details **1864 - Maine's Sovereignty Definition**
Sir Henry Maine prepared official definition:
- Concept of **sovereignty as applied to Indian States**
- Interpreted sovereignty as **divisible compilation of rights**
- **Implication**: Native States transferred some sovereign rights to British but retained others
:::

:::details **1864 - Emigration Act Update**
Act XIII passed:
- Updated provisions governing emigration of Indian labor overseas
- Governed:
  - Methods of recruitment
  - Treatment of emigrants in transit
  - Provision of 25 women for every 100 men
:::

:::details **1865 - Abolition of Grand Juries**
Maine acquired passage of Act XIII:
- **Abolished use of grand juries** in India
- In favor of petty juries
- **Belief**: Require Europeans in Mofussil more subject to prosecution for criminal acts
:::

:::details **1872 - Stephen's Evidence Contributions**
Stephen made significant contributions:

**Publications**:
- *Indian Evidence Act; With an Introduction on the Principles of Judicial Evidence* (1872)
- *Indian Code of Criminal Procedures* (1872)

**Proposals**:
- Judicial interrogation at committal proceedings
- Judicial interrogation at trials
:::

## üî• Ilbert Bill Controversy (1879-1882)

:::details **1879 - Fourth Law Commission**
Lord Lytton (1831-1891), Viceroy, created Commission:
- **Chairman**: **Whitney Stokes** (1830-1909)

**Commission Produced**:
- **1881**: Code of Negotiable Instruments
- **1882**: Codes for:
  - Trusts
  - Transfer of Property
  - Easements
  - Revised Criminal and Civil Procedures
:::

:::details **1879 - Deccan Agriculturists' Relief Act**
Broke precedent with free market principles:
- Provided judges power to **disregard specific contracts**
- To revise terms of cultivators' debts
:::

:::details **1882 - Ilbert Bill**
**Sir Courtenay P. Ilbert** (1841-1924) introduced bill:

**Initial Projection**:
- Possibility of **Indian judge passing judgement on European**

**Reaction**:
- **Enormous furor** from European community
- Violent British opposition besmirched liberal reputation

**Outcome**:
- Bill **greatly modified** and passed into law
- See Chapter 5 for details
- Demonstrated racial tensions in judicial system
:::

## üéì Legal Profession Advancement (1909-1926)

:::details **24 Mar 1909 - First Indian Law Member**
**Satyendra P. Sinha** (1864-1928):
- Became **first Indian Law Member** of Viceroy's Executive Council
- Landmark in Indian advancement in governance
:::

:::details **1922 - First Indian Woman Lawyer**
**Cornelia Sorabji** (1866-1954):
- Received law degree from **Oxford**
- **First Indian woman** to receive that distinction
- Completed degree work in 1892
- **24 Aug 1921**: High Court of Allahabad admitted her as vakil
- **Result of**: English Sex Disqualification (Removal) Act of 1919 spreading to India
:::

:::details **1923 - Indian Bar Council Committee**
Government of India named committee:
- **Led by**: **E. M. D. Chamier** (1866-1945)
- **Purpose**: Investigate:
  - Establishment of Indian bar on national or provincial basis
  - Distinctions between barristers and vakils

**1 Feb 1924 - Committee Report**:
- Recommended eliminating previous nomenclatures
- Entitle everyone as **"advocate"**
- Opted for **provincial bar councils** over centralized body

**9 Sept 1926 - Indian Bar Council's Act**:
- Lord Irwin, Viceroy, approved
- Prescribed:
  - Constitution of bar councils
  - Dealt with advocates
  - Elements of misconduct
  - Other matters
:::

:::details **2 Apr 1923 - Legal Practitioners' (Women) Act**
Act became law:
- Allowed **Indian women to practice law** as full-fledged lawyers in India
:::

:::details **1925 - Indian Succession Act**
Indian Succession Act passed:
- Superseded:
  - Indian Succession Act of 1865
  - Hindu Wills Act of 1870
  - Probate and Administration Act
:::

## üîë Quick Reference

**Major Institutions**:
- **1861**: High Courts established (Calcutta, Madras, Bombay)
- **1872**: High Court of Burma (defacto)
- **1926**: Provincial Bar Councils

**Landmark Legislation**:
- **1872**: Indian Contract Act, Evidence Act
- **1881**: Negotiable Instruments Code
- **1882**: Transfer of Property, Trusts, Easements
- **1925**: Indian Succession Act

**Controversial Moments**:
- **1882**: Ilbert Bill - Indian judges over Europeans
- Racial tensions in judicial appointments

**Indianization Milestones**:
- **1863**: Indian vakils in High Courts
- **1909**: First Indian Law Member (Sinha)
- **1921**: First Indian woman vakil (Sorabji)
- **1923**: Women allowed to practice law
- **1926**: Bar Council Act (advocates)

**Key Law Members**:
- **Sir Henry Maine** (1822-1888): Sovereignty definition, massive legislation
- **Sir James Fitzjames Stephen** (1829-1892): Evidence Act, Criminal Procedure
- **Sir Courtenay Ilbert** (1841-1924): Controversial Ilbert Bill
- **Satyendra P. Sinha** (1864-1928): First Indian Law Member

**Major Law Commissions**:
- **Third** (1862-1872): Contract, Evidence, Succession codes
- **Fourth** (1879-1882): Negotiable Instruments, Property laws

**Important Principles**:
- Unified High Courts replacing dual system
- Gradual Indianization of legal profession
- Modern codes replacing personal laws in many areas
- Women's entry into legal profession
- Professional bar organization

**Legacy**:
- High Courts system continued in independent India
- Codes formed basis of modern Indian law
- Legal profession became avenue for Indian advancement
- Indians carried forward rule of law to Republic of India
