## ü§ù SAARC - South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation

:::details **Introduction & Objectives**
**Formation:**
- Established: December 1985 in Dhaka
- Headquarters: Kathmandu, Nepal
- Members: 8 countries (Afghanistan, Bangladesh, Bhutan, India, Maldives, Nepal, Pakistan, Sri Lanka)
- Observers: 9 countries including China, US, EU, Japan

**Coverage:**
- 3% of world's land area
- 21% of global population (1.9 billion people)
- 4.21% of global GDP
- Least integrated region economically

**Objectives:**
- Promote welfare and quality of life in South Asia
- Accelerate economic growth and social progress
- Strengthen collective self-reliance
- Promote active collaboration in economic, social, cultural, technical fields
- Cooperation with international and regional organizations
:::

:::details **Significance of SAARC for India**
**Regional Integration:**
- Platform for India's neighborhood diplomacy
- Balance between globalization and nationalism
- Foster South Asian identity

**Socio-Economic Development:**
- Poverty alleviation initiatives
- Trade facilitation through SAFTA
- Development partnerships

**Security Cooperation:**
- Counter-terrorism framework
- Regional security architecture
- Disaster management cooperation

**Containing China:**
- Alternative to Chinese influence
- India-led regional cooperation
- Prevent BRI domination

**India's Global Ambitions:**
- Leadership in regional forum
- Credibility for global roles (UNSC)
- Soft power projection
:::

:::details **Achievements of SAARC**
**Economic:**
- SAPTA (1995): SAARC Preferential Trading Arrangement
- SAFTA (2006): South Asian Free Trade Area
- Reduced tariffs among members
- Intra-regional trade promotion

**Institutional:**
- South Asian University (New Delhi)
- SAARC Development Fund (SDF)
- SAARC Arbitration Council
- SAARC Cultural Centre (Colombo)

**Social:**
- Common South Asian identity initiatives
- Youth exchanges and scholarships
- Cultural programs

**COVID-19 Response:**
- SAARC COVID-19 Emergency Fund ($21.8 million)
- Video conference of leaders (March 2020)
- Medical equipment sharing
- Vaccine diplomacy platform
:::

:::details **Failures of SAARC**
**India-Pakistan Hostility:**
- Summit postponements due to tensions
- Uri attack (2016) - Summit boycotted
- Last summit: 2014 in Kathmandu
- 7+ years without leaders meeting

**Weak Implementation:**
- SAFTA limitations: Sensitive lists, negative lists
- South Asia least integrated region (intra-regional trade <5%)
- Most decisions remain on paper
- Lack of political will

**Consensus Decision-Making:**
- Pakistan blocks connectivity initiatives
- Single country veto power
- Slow decision-making process
- National interests override regional good

**Trust Deficit:**
- Border disputes unresolved
- River water conflicts
- Bilateral issues dominate
- Fear of Indian dominance

**Chinese Factor:**
- China's observer status creates complications
- BRI undermining SAARC projects
- Members turning to China for aid
- Geopolitical competition

**Limited Scope:**
- No customs union or common market
- Weak institutional mechanisms
- Limited budget and resources
- Secretariat has minimal powers
:::

:::details **Way Forward**
- **Reviving SAARC**: Critical for regional integration
- **Bilateral Track**: Resolve India-Pakistan issues separately
- **People-to-People**: Citizen diplomacy through social media
- **Economic Focus**: Make SAFTA effective
- **Technology**: E-governance for easier coordination
- **SAARC vs BIMSTEC**: Not either-or, both needed
- **Political Will**: Leadership commitment essential
:::

## üåç BRICS

:::details **Introduction & Formation**
**Origin:**
- Term coined: 2001 by Jim O'Neill (Goldman Sachs economist)
- Initial: BRIC (Brazil, Russia, India, China)
- South Africa joined: 2010 (became BRICS)
- First Summit: 2009 in Yekaterinburg, Russia

**Objectives:**
- Promote peace, security, development, cooperation
- Reform global financial architecture
- Equitable international order
- Represent emerging economies

**Global Significance:**
- 41% of world population (3.2+ billion)
- 24% of global GDP
- 16% of global trade
- 31.5% of world GDP (PPP) vs G7's 30.7%
:::

:::details **BRICS Mechanisms & Institutions**
**New Development Bank (NDB):**
- Established: 2014, Shanghai headquarters
- Capital: $100 billion
- Focus: Infrastructure and sustainable development
- Equal voting rights (unlike IMF/World Bank)
- India's projects: Renewable energy, metro

**Contingent Reserve Arrangement (CRA):**
- $100 billion pool
- Currency crisis protection
- Alternative to IMF assistance
- Shield against financial shocks

**BRICS Business Council:**
- Promote trade and investment
- Remove barriers
- Facilitate business partnerships

**Think Tank Council:**
- Policy recommendations
- Research cooperation
- Knowledge sharing
:::

:::details **Significance of BRICS for India**
**Economic Opportunities:**
- Market access to 3+ billion consumers
- Trade: $110 billion with BRICS (2019-20)
- Investment opportunities
- Technology transfer

**Geopolitical Influence:**
- Voice in global governance
- Reform UN Security Council
- Challenge Western dominance
- Multi-polar world order

**Developmental Cooperation:**
- NDB loans for infrastructure
- Knowledge sharing on growth models
- South-South cooperation
- Technology partnerships

**Strategic Autonomy:**
- Reduce dependence on Western institutions
- Alternative to US-led order
- Balanced foreign policy
- Non-aligned in spirit
:::

:::details **14th BRICS Summit (2022)**
**Theme:** "Foster High-quality BRICS Partnership, Usher in a New Era for Global Development"

**Key Outcomes:**
- Beijing Declaration adopted
- Support for multilateral system reform
- COVID-19 pandemic cooperation
- Global economic recovery discussed
- Counter-terrorism cooperation enhanced

**India's Proposals:**
- Online database for BRICS research
- BRICS Railways Research Network
- MSME cooperation mechanism
- Disaster resilience infrastructure

**Ukraine Issue:**
- Humanitarian assistance support
- ICRC and UN role endorsed
- Varied positions among members
:::

:::details **Issues and Challenges**
**China's Dominance:**
- Unilateralism concerns
- Chinese agenda driving group
- Imbalance in economic weight
- Belt and Road Initiative overlap

**Geopolitical Differences:**
- Russia-Ukraine: India abstained, China backed Russia, Brazil neutral
- Indo-Pacific: India-China tensions
- Border conflicts: India-China LAC issue
- Different UN voting patterns

**Trade Imbalances:**
- India's huge deficit with China
- Non-tariff barriers
- Market access issues
- Lack of equal opportunities

**Political Systems:**
- Democracies (India, Brazil, South Africa) vs Authoritarian (China, Russia)
- Different values and priorities
- Human rights divergences
- Governance models clash

**Expansion Debate:**
- 20+ countries seeking membership
- Argentina, Iran, Saudi Arabia interested
- Consensus building challenges
- Dilution of group identity

**Limited Institutional Framework:**
- No permanent secretariat
- Weak enforcement mechanisms
- Ad-hoc cooperation
- Competing bilateral interests
:::

:::details **Way Forward**
- **Balanced Approach**: Don't let China dominate
- **Economic Focus**: Trade facilitation, reduce barriers
- **NDB Strengthening**: More projects in India
- **Technology Cooperation**: Digital economy, AI, space
- **Pragmatic Engagement**: Issue-based coalitions
- **Reform Advocacy**: Continue pushing for UN, IMF reforms
- **Expansion Criteria**: Clear framework for new members
:::

## üåä BIMSTEC - Bay of Bengal Initiative

:::details **Introduction**
**Formation:**
- Established: 1997 in Bangkok
- Members: 7 countries (Bangladesh, Bhutan, India, Myanmar, Nepal, Sri Lanka, Thailand)
- Headquarters: Dhaka, Bangladesh
- Bridges South and Southeast Asia

**Principles:**
- Sovereign equality
- Non-interference
- Consensus-based decision making
- Gradual and incremental approach
:::

:::details **Importance of BIMSTEC for India**
**Geopolitical:**
- Links South Asia with Southeast Asia
- Act East Policy implementation
- Alternative to SAARC (Pakistan-free)
- Counter China's influence

**Economic:**
- Combined GDP: $3.5 trillion
- 1.7 billion population (22% of global)
- Market access to ASEAN
- Maritime cooperation in Bay of Bengal

**Connectivity:**
- Motor vehicles agreement potential
- Coastal shipping
- Air connectivity
- Digital highways

**Security:**
- Counter-terrorism cooperation
- Maritime security
- Disaster management
- Transnational crime
:::

:::details **Areas of Cooperation (7 Priority Sectors)**
1. **Trade and Investment**
2. **Technology**
3. **Energy**
4. **Transport and Communication**
5. **Tourism**
6. **Fisheries**
7. **Agriculture**

**Recent Additions:**
- Climate change
- People-to-people contacts
- Culture
- Counter-terrorism and transnational crime
:::

:::details **Issues & Challenges**
**Lack of Progress:**
- No Free Trade Agreement yet
- Limited institutional framework
- Slow decision-making
- Weak secretariat

**Diverse Priorities:**
- South Asian vs Southeast Asian focus
- Development gaps between members
- Different economic models
- Competing interests

**Connectivity Gaps:**
- Infrastructure deficits
- Funding constraints
- Cross-border challenges
- Coordination issues

**Myanmar Crisis:**
- Military coup impact
- Refugee crisis
- Democracy concerns
- Affects group cohesion
:::

:::details **Way Forward**
- **FTA Acceleration**: Complete trade agreement
- **Connectivity Focus**: Trilateral highway, coastal shipping
- **Institutional Strengthening**: Permanent secretariat empowerment
- **Regular Summits**: Annual leaders meetings
- **India's Leadership**: Proactive role in pushing agenda
- **BBIN Model**: Sub-regional projects first
- **Maritime Cooperation**: Bay of Bengal focus areas
:::

## üåè ASEAN & India

:::details **Introduction to ASEAN**
**Formation:**
- Established: 1967, Bangkok Declaration
- Members: 10 Southeast Asian countries
- Headquarters: Jakarta, Indonesia
- Population: 650+ million
- Combined GDP: $3 trillion

**Fundamental Principles:**
- Mutual respect for sovereignty
- Non-interference in internal affairs
- Peaceful settlement of disputes
- Effective cooperation
:::

:::details **Importance of ASEAN for India**
**Strategic:**
- Act East Policy cornerstone
- Counter-balance to China
- Indo-Pacific cooperation
- Maritime security partner

**Economic:**
- 4th largest trading partner (after US, China, EU)
- Trade: $87.4 billion (2021-22)
- Investment destination for Indian companies
- Regional value chains integration

**Connectivity:**
- Links to East Asia
- ASEAN-India maritime cooperation
- Trilateral highway
- Digital connectivity

**Security:**
- Counter-terrorism cooperation
- ASEAN Defence Ministers Meeting Plus
- Naval exercises
- Disaster relief coordination
:::

:::details **India-ASEAN Relations Evolution**
**Milestones:**
- **1992**: Sectoral Dialogue Partner
- **1995**: Full Dialogue Partner
- **2002**: Summit-level partnership
- **2012**: Strategic Partnership
- **2022**: Comprehensive Strategic Partnership

**Mechanisms:**
- Annual Summits
- Foreign Ministers meetings
- Trade and Investment meetings
- ASEAN Regional Forum participation
:::

:::details **Issues and Challenges**
**RCEP Exit:**
- India opted out in 2019
- Trade deficit concerns
- Domestic industry protection
- Lost market access opportunities

**South China Sea:**
- ASEAN divided on China
- India's balancing act
- Freedom of navigation support
- Limited leverage

**Myanmar Crisis:**
- Military coup complications
- ASEAN's ineffective response
- India's strategic interests
- Democracy vs. stability dilemma

**Trade Barriers:**
- Non-tariff barriers persist
- Services sector access limited
- Rules of origin issues
- Incomplete FTA implementation
:::

:::details **Way Forward**
- **Deepen Engagement**: Beyond trade to technology
- **Maritime Cooperation**: SAGAR initiative implementation
- **Connectivity Projects**: Complete Trilateral Highway
- **Digital Partnership**: IT, cyber security cooperation
- **Defence Ties**: Joint exercises, capacity building
- **RCEP Reconsideration**: Evaluate re-entry with safeguards
- **ASEAN Centrality**: Support ASEAN-led mechanisms
:::

## üî∑ QUAD - Quadrilateral Security Dialogue

:::details **Introduction & Formation**
**Origins:**
- Proposed: 2004 post-tsunami cooperation
- Formalized: 2007 under Japanese PM Abe
- Revived: 2017 on sidelines of ASEAN
- First Leaders Summit: March 2021 (virtual)

**Members:**
- India
- United States
- Japan
- Australia

**Nature:**
- Informal strategic forum
- Not a military alliance
- Flexible coalition
- Issue-based cooperation
:::

:::details **Objectives & Focus Areas**
**Primary Goals:**
- Free and open Indo-Pacific
- Rules-based international order
- Freedom of navigation
- Respect for territorial integrity
- Peaceful dispute resolution

**Cooperation Areas:**
- COVID-19 response (Quad Vaccine Partnership)
- Climate change
- Critical and emerging technologies
- Infrastructure
- Cyber security
- Maritime security
:::

:::details **Importance for Members**
**For India:**
- Balance China without confrontation
- Strategic autonomy maintained
- Technology partnerships
- Maritime security cooperation
- Vaccine diplomacy platform

**For US:**
- Counter Chinese assertiveness
- Allies/partners network
- Indo-Pacific strategy
- Democratic values promotion

**For Japan & Australia:**
- China containment
- Supply chain resilience
- Regional stability
- Economic security
:::

:::details **Issues & Challenges**
**China's Opposition:**
- Labels it "Asian NATO"
- Accuses of Cold War mentality
- Diplomatic pressure on members
- Economic coercion threats

**India's Strategic Autonomy:**
- Not a military alliance for India
- Balanced relations with Russia
- Non-alignment legacy
- Domestic political sensitivities

**Diverse Priorities:**
- US focus on military aspects
- India on connectivity and development
- Different threat perceptions
- Economic interests vary

**AUKUS Impact:**
- Separate US-UK-Australia security pact
- Nuclear submarine deal
- France angered
- Quad's clarity questioned
:::

:::details **Way Forward**
- **Positive Agenda**: Focus on development, not just containment
- **Vaccine Diplomacy**: Continue pharmaceutical cooperation
- **Technology Sharing**: 5G, AI, quantum computing
- **Infrastructure**: Quality alternatives to BRI
- **Climate Action**: Clean energy initiatives
- **Maritime Security**: Coast guard cooperation
- **Avoid Militarization**: Keep strategic autonomy
:::

## üåê SCO - Shanghai Cooperation Organization

:::details **Introduction**
**Formation:**
- Established: 2001 in Shanghai
- Predecessor: Shanghai Five (1996)
- Headquarters: Beijing, China
- Secretariat: Tashkent, Uzbekistan

**Members (9):**
- China, Russia, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Uzbekistan
- India and Pakistan (2017)
- Iran (2023)

**Observers:** Afghanistan, Belarus, Mongolia

**Dialogue Partners:** Turkey, Sri Lanka, others
:::

:::details **Objectives and Principles**
**Core Objectives:**
- Combat "three evil forces": Terrorism, Separatism, Extremism
- Promote regional security
- Economic cooperation
- Cultural exchanges

**Shanghai Spirit:**
- Mutual trust
- Mutual benefit
- Equality
- Consultation
- Respect for diversity
- Pursuit of common development
:::

:::details **Importance of SCO for India**
**Central Asia Access:**
- Direct engagement platform
- Energy cooperation
- Trade facilitation
- Connectivity projects

**Counter-Terrorism:**
- Regional Anti-Terrorist Structure (RATS)
- Intelligence sharing
- Joint operations
- Combat extremism

**Geopolitical Balance:**
- Engage China and Russia
- Strategic autonomy
- Multipolar world order
- Alternative to Western-led forums

**Economic Opportunities:**
- Eurasian markets access
- Energy resources
- Trade facilitation
- Investment opportunities
:::

:::details **Challenges for India in SCO**
**China-Pakistan Axis:**
- Both members with India
- CPEC issues
- Border tensions with China
- Terrorism from Pakistan

**Russia-China Convergence:**
- Increasing bilateral cooperation
- India's balancing act
- Different priorities
- Strategic competition

**Afghanistan Issue:**
- Taliban recognition debate
- Terror sanctuaries
- Regional stability
- Competing interests

**Limited Economic Integration:**
- No FTA framework
- Trade barriers persist
- Connectivity gaps
- Implementation challenges
:::

:::details **Way Forward**
- **Pragmatic Engagement**: Issue-based cooperation
- **Counter-Terrorism**: Use RATS effectively
- **Energy Security**: Central Asian partnerships
- **Chabahar-INSTC**: Link to SCO projects
- **Balance China**: Use Russia relations
- **Afghanistan Coordination**: Regional solution
- **Economic Focus**: Push for trade facilitation
:::
